---
name: quiz-me
description: Quiz the user with free-response questions about a concept they've just been learning, drawn from the current conversation, to test and cement their understanding. Grade each answer honestly, then push past what was explicitly covered into adjacent ideas that are critical to really understanding the topic. Use when the user says "quiz me", wants to test their grasp of something they've just learned or discussed, or wants to check whether a new concept actually landed.
---

Quiz me with free-response questions about the topic we've just been discussing. Treat this conversation as the study material — the things I asked about, the explanations you gave, and the mental models we built up.

Ask one question at a time and wait for my answer before moving on. Don't dump a list, and don't give the answer away inside the question. Free response only — no multiple choice. The point is to make me actually articulate the idea, not recognize it.

After each answer, grade it honestly. Tell me what I got right, what I missed, and correct anything that's subtly off. I'm here to learn, so flattery is worse than useless — waving through a half-right answer just costs me a question and leaves me with a false sense of understanding. Be direct, but constructive: when I'm wrong, show me the gap, don't just mark it wrong. And make sure your corrections are actually correct — if you're not certain of a fact, verify it (codebase, docs, web) rather than confidently telling me something false, because a wrong "correction" in a quiz does real damage.

Order the questions to build. Start with the core ideas we actually covered to confirm they landed. Then work outward — once I've shown I've got the basics, start asking questions that bridge from what we discussed to the things we *didn't* cover but that are genuinely critical to understanding this properly. Frame those so I have to reason my way across the gap from what I know, rather than recall a fact I was handed. That's where most of the real learning happens, so don't skip it just because we never explicitly talked about it.

Adapt as you go. Push deeper when I'm solid; scaffold, narrow the question, or drop a hint when I'm stuck. Keep the difficulty high enough that it's a real test and not a softball — if I'm getting everything instantly, you're going too easy.

Keep going until we've covered the concept thoroughly or I tell you to stop. When we wrap, give me a short read-out: what I clearly have down, and what's worth reviewing.
